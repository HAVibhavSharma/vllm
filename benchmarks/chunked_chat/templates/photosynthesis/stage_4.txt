Student: What is photosynthesis, in one sentence?
Tutor: Photosynthesis is the process by which plants, algae, and some bacteria
use light energy to convert carbon dioxide and water into glucose and oxygen.
Key terms: chloroplast, chlorophyll, glucose

Student: Where in the plant does this actually happen?
Tutor: Photosynthesis takes place primarily in the mesophyll cells of leaves,
inside organelles called chloroplasts. The light-dependent reactions occur on
the thylakoid membranes, while the Calvin cycle takes place in the surrounding
stroma. Stomatal pores on the leaf surface regulate the gas exchange that both
stages depend on.
Key terms: mesophyll, thylakoid, stroma, stomata

Student: Can you walk me through the light-dependent reactions step by step?
Tutor: In the thylakoid membrane, photosystem II absorbs photons and excites
electrons, which travel down an electron transport chain while protons are
pumped into the thylakoid lumen. Photosystem I re-energizes those electrons so
they can reduce NADP+ into NADPH. The proton gradient built up in the lumen
drives ATP synthase to produce ATP. Water is split at photosystem II to replace
the electrons lost upstream, releasing molecular oxygen as a byproduct.
Key terms: photosystem II, photosystem I, NADPH, ATP synthase
